Attentional modulation of functional connectivity in the face processing network of the brain - Poster RAW 2023

Abstract

Attention can modulate the responses of our brain. However, the effect attention can have on the face processing network in different frequency bands has yet to be fully understood. The present study investigates the attentional modulation of the brain's neuronal activity and functional connectivity during face processing. Task-based magnetoencephalography (MEG) was recorded from ten participants. The participants attended to compound facial stimuli and responded either to parts of the face (eyes or mouth) or the facial identity of the presented face stimulus. Each condition's relative activation and functional connectivity were analysed in theta, alpha, beta, and gamma frequency bands. For this, ten regions of interest related to visuospatial attention and face processing were selected. The results indicate decreased activity when attending to the mouth part in the right frontal eye field (FEF) in the theta and beta frequency bands and involvement of the middle temporal visual area between the identity and mouth condition in the alpha and gamma bands and a decreased activation of the perirhinal ectorhinal cortex during attention to the face-identity. An increase in functional connectivity in the alpha band when attending to the facial identity between the right FEF and the perirhinal ectorhinal cortex was found. In the alpha band, the data suggests an increase between the right IFJa and the right superior temporal sulcus (STS) when attending to the eyes. The results show an increase in the functional connectivity in the beta band between the right anterior inferior frontal junction (IFJ) and the STS and a decrease between the anterior IFJ and the right FEF. The results suggest different attentionbased modulations of brain activity and functional connectivity in several brain areas related to processing different face features in multiple frequency bands. The IFJ and the FEF might be crucial in modulating these areas' attention.
